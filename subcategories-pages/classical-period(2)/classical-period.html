Classical-period

The Classical period in Western music (approximately 1750–1820) is a defining era in the history of art music, centered on clarity, balance, and structural elegance. It followed the ornate Baroque style and moved toward simpler melodies supported by clear harmonic progressions. Major composers of this era — such as Joseph Haydn, Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, and Ludwig van Beethoven — shaped new musical forms like the symphony, sonata, concerto, and string quartet. Music from this period emphasizes formal balance, well‑defined phrases, and emotional restraint, creating a sense of order and accessibility. Public concerts became more common, meaning music was not only for courts but for wider audiences. Instrumentation in orchestras became more standardized, with expanded wind sections complementing strings to achieve richer textures.
